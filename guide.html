01Before You Ride

Start it right,
every single time.

Starting your bike, step by step

Turn the key. Push the small red switch to "ON." You may hear a soft whirrr for two seconds — that's normal, it's just the bike getting ready. Wait for it to go quiet, then press the start button. That's it.

Don't rev it the moment it starts

When a bike sits overnight, the oil settles at the bottom. If you rev hard straight away, the engine runs dry for a moment and wears out faster. Just wait a few seconds before you go. Easy.

You don't need to "warm up" for 5 minutes. Old advice says let the bike sit and run before riding. New bikes don't need that. Start it, wait 20–30 seconds, then ride slowly for the first minute. The bike warms up best while you ride.
A quick 2-minute check before you go

Press the Tyres

They should feel hard, not soft.

Test the Brakes

Squeeze both. They should grip.

Look Below

No oily puddles under the bike.

Helmet On

All good? Now you ride.

02Your Motorcycle in One Page

From the tank
to the tyre.

No heavy engineering. Just enough to understand what happens when you twist the throttle (the handle grip you turn to go faster).

Fuel Tank
Holds the petrol.
Fuel Pump
Pushes the petrol forward.
Injector
Mixes petrol with air into a fine spray.
Engine
A tiny spark lights the spray. The small blast pushes the engine.
Clutch
The lever in your left hand. Squeeze it to pause the power when changing gears.
Gearbox
Low gears give power to start. High gears give speed.
Chain / Belt
Carries the power to the back wheel.
Rear Wheel
It spins, and the bike moves.
In
Short

Twisting the throttle doesn't just say "go faster." It lets more petrol and air into the engine. Bigger blasts, faster engine, more speed. That's the whole secret.

03The 5-Minute Weekly Check

Pick one day.
Sunday is perfect.

Six quick checks save you from getting stuck on the road. Do them when the bike is cold (before riding).

Tyre Pressure

Low air makes the bike heavy and unsafe. Check when cold.

Chain

Not too dry, not too loose. About one finger of slack is right.

Engine Oil

Hold the bike straight up. Oil should sit between the two lines.

Brake Fluid

Look at the small window near the handle. Dark colour means change it.

Lights

Headlight, indicators, and brake light. All should work.

Horn

One quick beep. If it works, you're done.

Why check when cold? Tyres heat up while you ride, and warm tyres show a higher number than the real one. So always check before you start riding, not after.

04Fuel Basics

What actually
goes in the tank.

Which petrol to use

Do you need expensive "premium" petrol? For most everyday bikes, no. Use the normal petrol your bike's manual asks for. Premium won't make a regular bike faster — it only empties your wallet.

About ethanol

Today's petrol has a little ethanol mixed in. It's fine to ride with. The only catch: it slowly pulls water from the air, which can cause trouble if the bike sits unused.

Don't let the bike sit for months

If a bike is parked for many months, the petrol goes stale and turns sticky, blocking the fuel parts. If you must park it long-term, add a cheap "fuel stabiliser" to the tank first.

Full tank or half?

Parking a metal-tank bike for a long time? Fill it fully. Empty space inside the tank lets water form and causes rust.

"Reserve" is not a secret fuel. Many beginners think reserve is a hidden extra tank. It isn't. It's the same tank — there are just two pipes inside. The normal pipe sits higher; the reserve pipe sits lower. When the higher pipe runs dry, you switch to reserve to use the last bit at the bottom. Time to refuel soon!

05Common Mistakes

The habits that
cost you money.

✕

Riding with low tyre air

The bike feels heavy and slips more in turns. Keep the air right.

✕

Never cleaning the chain

A dirty chain damages parts that cost a lot to replace. A cleaning spray is cheap.

✕

Washing the engine while it's hot

Cold water on a hot engine can crack it. Let the bike cool down first.

✕

Making the chain too tight

The chain needs a little slack. Too tight and it can snap while riding.

✕

Waiting for something to break

Small problems grow into big ones. Fix little things early, before they leave you stuck.

The simple idea: a little care now saves a big bill later. Looking after your bike is cheaper than fixing it.

06Riding Gear Priority

Buy in this
exact order.

Can't buy all your safety gear at once? No problem. Buy it in this order — most important first.

1

Helmet

The most important thing. It protects your head. Buy one with an ISI, DOT, or ECE safety mark.

2

Gloves

In a fall, your hands hit the ground first. Gloves protect your skin and grip.

3

Shoes / Boots

Your ankles are easy to hurt. Strong shoes that cover the ankle keep them safe.

4

Jacket

A riding jacket has padding at the back, shoulders, and elbows to protect you in a fall.

5

Pants

Normal jeans tear easily on the road. Riding pants are stronger and protect your legs.

What lives
under your seat.

You don't need a full toolbox. Just keep these small things under your seat. They can save a long, stressful wait on the road.

A small battery pump to fill a low tyre.

Air Checker

To check tyre air correctly.

Zip Ties

Quick fix for loose or hanging parts.

Basic Tools

The small tool kit your bike came with.

Torch

For fixing things in the dark.

First Aid

Bandages, wipes, and pain tablets.

The Habit

09Myth vs Fact

Setting the
record straight.

✕ Myth

You must warm up the bike for 10 minutes before riding.

✓ Fact

New bikes don't need that. Wait 20–30 seconds, then ride slowly at first.

✕ Myth

Expensive petrol makes a normal bike faster.

✓ Fact

It doesn't. Just use the petrol your bike's manual asks for.

✕ Myth

More engine oil is better.

✓ Fact

Too much oil causes leaks. Fill only up to the line.

✕ Myth

Use mostly the back brake so you don't fall.

✓ Fact

The front brake does most of the stopping. Use both, and squeeze the front gently.
