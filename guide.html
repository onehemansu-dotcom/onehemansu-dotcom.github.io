Before You Read This

Buying a bike is easy.
Owning one properly
is harder.

Most expensive repairs don't happen because someone crashed. They happen because small warning signs were ignored.

A slightly loose chain. Tyres that stayed low on air for months. An engine that always ran with the wrong oil level. A strange vibration everyone assumed was "normal."

Bikes rarely stay healthy by accident. They stay healthy because their owners notice small changes before they become expensive problems.

This guide won't make you a mechanic. It'll help you become the kind of owner who knows when something isn't right.

OG Ladka 01
Bad Habits

7 things that quietly
shorten your bike's life.

1

Skipping the first service

New engine parts wear into each other and drop tiny metal bits into the oil. The first service cleans all of that out. Skip it and you're hurting the engine at its most important stage.

2

Running the tank almost empty every time

Dirt and water settle at the bottom of your tank. Riding on reserve pushes all of that into the engine. Fill up before you hit empty.

3

Filling oil above the max mark

More oil doesn't mean more protection. Too much oil turns into foam. Foam doesn't lubricate. Keep it between the MIN and MAX lines.

4

Waiting until the chain gets noisy

By the time it's noisy, the damage is already happening. Regular cleaning and oiling is much cheaper than replacing the whole chain kit.

5

Idling the engine for several minutes

Old bikes needed this. Yours probably doesn't. Wait 20–30 seconds, then ride gently. That warms it up better than sitting still.

6

Never checking tyre pressure

Low pressure kills grip, eats fuel, wears tyres fast, and makes the bike feel unstable. One minute to check. That's all.

7

Ignoring small problems because "it still runs"

Small problems become expensive problems after being ignored for weeks. The earlier you catch it, the cheaper the fix.

OG Ladka 02
Pay Attention

Your bike is always
giving you information.

Bikes don't speak. But they constantly communicate. Learn what they're telling you.

Listen

New clicking, rattling, grinding, or whining? Something has changed. Worth checking.

Feel

Handlebars, footpegs, clutch, or seat feel different? Vibration changes show up before failures.

Smell

Burning oil, burning clutch, fuel, burning plastic. None of these should be ignored.

Look

Oil stains, coolant leaks, loose bolts, uneven tyre wear. A quick look before riding takes one minute.

The Goal

Don't be paranoid. Just know what feels normal. When something changes, find out why.

OG Ladka 03
Weekly Routine

5 things. 5 minutes.
Once a week.

You don't need to inspect every bolt. Just spend five minutes.

Tyres

Check pressure when cold. Low pressure affects grip, braking, and tyre life.

Chain

Check slack. If it's dirty, clean it. If it's dry, oil it.

Brakes

Both brakes should feel firm. Soft or spongy? Get it checked.

Lights & Horn

Headlight, brake light, indicators, horn. Takes 30 seconds.

Fluids

Check engine oil level. If your bike uses coolant, check that too.

Five minutes won't prevent every problem. But they help catch most issues before they become expensive repairs.
OG Ladka 04
Myth vs Reality

Things people keep
saying that aren't true.

✕ Myth

More engine oil protects the engine better.

✓ Reality

Too much oil turns into foam. Foam doesn't lubricate well and can damage seals.

✕ Myth

Premium petrol always makes a bike faster.

✓ Reality

Only engines built for high-octane fuel benefit from it. Most commuter bikes don't need it.

✕ Myth

You should idle the bike for 10 minutes before riding.

✓ Reality

Modern bikes warm up better when ridden gently. Long idling wastes fuel and time.

✕ Myth

The rear brake is the safest brake.

✓ Reality

Weight shifts forward when braking. The front brake does most of the stopping. Use both together.

✕ Myth

A chain only needs oil when it's noisy.

✓ Reality

Noise means you're already late. Oil the chain before it asks for it.
